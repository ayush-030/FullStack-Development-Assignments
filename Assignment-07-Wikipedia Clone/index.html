Peafowl

Peafowl is a common name for two bird species of the genus Pavo and one species of the closely related genus Afropavo within the tribe Pavonini of the family Phasianidae (the pheasants and their allies). Male peafowl are referred to as peacocks, and female peafowl as peahens.
The two Asiatic species are the Indian peafowl and green peafowl. The third is the Congo peafowl. Male peafowl are known for their extravagant plumage and piercing calls.

Description

The Indian peacock (Pavo cristatus) has iridescent blue and green plumage, with males notably longer than females due to their tail coverts, called a 'train', covered in eyespots. Peahens (females) are duller, with grey, brown, and green coloring. Green peafowl males (Pavo muticus) have green and gold plumage with bluish-black wings, and both males and females have spurs. The Congo peacock (Afropavo congensis) male uses his actual tail feathers, which are much shorter and less colorful, unlike the elaborate feather displays by Indian and green peafowls.

Iridescence and structural coloration

The brilliant colors of peafowl plumage are mainly structural, not pigment-based. Optical interference from nanostructures in feather barbules creates the characteristic iridescence, changing with viewing angle. Brown feathers are created from a mix of red and blue caused by these structural effects.

Courtship

In courtship displays, the peacock places himself at an ideal angle to sunlight to best show off his iridescent train to the peahen. The train is fanned and vibrated to draw her attention, with females evaluating not only train size but also coloration and display angle.

Evolution and selection

Charles Darwin proposed that peacock trains evolved through sexual selection, while the "handicap principle" suggests brilliant trains honestly signal male fitness. Different studies show both train display and other behaviors affect mate choice.

Diet

Peafowl are omnivores, eating plants, flower petals, insects, reptiles, amphibians, and more. They forage mostly in the early morning or at dusk, and are not picky in what they eat.

Cultural significance

The peafowl features prominently in multiple cultures: It is India's national bird (and sacred in Hinduism), appears in Buddhist and ancient Greek stories, and is associated with immortality in Christianity due to its beautiful plumage.
